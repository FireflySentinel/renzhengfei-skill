# External Views, Criticism, and Analysis by Others

Research dimension: How scholars, business analysts, employees, and the international management community assess Ren Zhengfei's leadership and management philosophy.

---

## 1. Academic Analysis of His Management Philosophy

### Harvard Business School Case Study

The HBS case study "Huawei the Ren Zhengfei Way: the 'Tough Guy' and His Corporate Philosophy" (CB0099, 2018) by Jiing-Lih Larry Farh and Xin Pi at CEIBS examines how Ren's leadership embodies "paternalistic leadership" -- a style characterized by clear-cut goals, hard-and-fast rules, employee dedication and absolute obedience, paired with generous remuneration for contributors. The case frames two central management principles that Ren codified from 1997 onward: customer centricity and total employee dedication to corporate goals. It also traces a deliberate shift: after a pronounced period of centralization (1997-2003), Huawei decentralized power and established processes where strategic questions are debated rather than decided solely by the CEO. [Source: analysis]

- Source: [HBS Case CB0099](https://hbsp.harvard.edu/product/CB0099-PDF-ENG)
- Source: [CEIBS Repository](https://repository.ceibs.cn/en/publications/huawei-the-ren-zhengfei-way-the-tough-guy-and-his-corporate-philo/)

### Cambridge University Press

"The Management Transformation of Huawei" (Cambridge University Press) documents how Ren's 1998 visit to IBM, HP, and Bell Labs catalyzed a wholesale adoption of Western management systems. He hired IBM consulting -- seventy consultants for five years at a total cost of roughly $291 million -- insisting that Huawei copy Western practices exactly unless the consultants themselves approved modifications. Over subsequent years, Huawei's product development was designed by IBM, HR by Hay Group, organizational structure by Mercer, sales by Accenture, and supply chain by IBM again. The book argues this willingness to subordinate Chinese management instincts to proven Western systems was a turning point in Huawei becoming a world-class company. [Source: analysis]

- Source: [Cambridge Core - Management Transformation of Huawei](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/management-transformation-of-huawei/management-transformation-of-huawei/F5888208EC556E03072E71574D0653B1)

### SAGE / De Cremer Research

David De Cremer (KPMG Professor at Cambridge Judge Business School), Tian Tao, and Wu Chunbo co-authored "Huawei: Leadership, Culture, and Connectivity" (SAGE, 2017), based on 14 years of research and 136 senior management and employee interviews. Their central finding: Huawei's success lies in its ability to "transform the intellectual elite into a band of soldiers with the same set of values and resolve, while at the same time preventing a culture of subservience." They characterize Ren as a leader who inspires innovation at all levels while adopting a reflective, self-criticizing attitude, especially in times of success. [Source: analysis]

- Source: [SAGE Publication](https://us2.sagepub.com/sites/default/files/upm-assets/76900_book_item_76900.pdf)

## 2. How Management Scholars Characterize His Leadership Style

Scholars consistently identify three overlapping frameworks:

**Paternalistic Leadership.** The dominant academic classification. Ren integrated strict military-style discipline (clear goals, sacrifice, obedience) with a doctrine of "taking care of the troops" -- rewarding staff with high pay and stock options. He demanded loyalty and performance yet cultivated a familial culture. The Financial Times has noted that paternalistic leadership, as exemplified by Ren, "offers lessons for Western executives." [Source: analysis]

**Transformational Leadership.** Some analysts identify transformational elements: Ren acts as a teacher, sharing book recommendations, discussing tactics, and providing life lessons, elevating employee capabilities to better serve customers. His inspirational style guided Huawei through multiple transformational phases. However, Ren himself downplays this, noting he "may not be that good in bringing people together as many would like to believe." [Source: analysis]

**Mao-Influenced Strategic Thinking.** Harvard Business Review (2007) documented Mao Zedong's pervasive influence on Chinese CEOs, with Ren as a prime example. While serving in the PLA, Ren extensively studied Mao's writings, and his strategic thinking was shaped by Mao's approaches -- particularly "criticism and self-criticism" sessions, surrounding-the-cities-from-the-countryside market strategy, and the belief that people who love their work will be devoted without transactional motivation. Scholars note Ren's effort to "extract the essence and discard the dross" from Mao's thought. [Source: analysis]

- Source: [European Business Review - Seven Leadership Lessons](https://www.europeanbusinessreview.com/leading-huawei-seven-leadership-lessons-of-ren-zhengfei/)
- Source: [European Financial Review](https://www.europeanfinancialreview.com/leadership-is-about-making-others-smarter-to-better-serve-customers/)
- Source: [HBR - Mao's Pervasive Influence on Chinese CEOs](https://hbr.org/2007/12/maos-pervasive-influence-on-chinese-ceos)

## 3. Comparisons with Other Business Leaders

**Jack Ma (Alibaba).** The most frequent comparison in Chinese business literature. Where Ma is the charismatic showman and platform builder, Ren is the disciplined engineer and hardware manufacturer. Ma courted publicity; Ren avoided it for decades. Ma built an ecosystem through partnerships; Ren built vertically integrated capability through R&D investment. Analysts characterize this as the "internet economy vs. deep tech" divide in Chinese entrepreneurship. [Source: analysis/opinion]

**Andy Grove (Intel).** Both share military-influenced discipline, paranoia about competition ("only the paranoid survive" echoes Ren's crisis rhetoric), demanding management styles, and willingness to berate underperformers. Both drove their companies through technology transitions by force of will. [Source: analysis]

**Fortune Rankings.** Fortune named Ren China's most influential business leader, ranking him ahead of Lenovo founder Liu Chuanzhi and Haier CEO Zhang Ruimin. He appeared on Fortune's 100 Most Powerful People in Business list in 2024. [Source: factual]

- Source: [Fortune - Most Powerful People](https://fortune.com/ranking/most-powerful-people/2024/ren-zhengfei/)

## 4. Criticism of His Management Approach

### Wolf Culture and Its Human Cost

Huawei's self-described "wolf culture" -- teams expected to act with the "intensity of a wolf pack" -- is the most criticized element. The average Huawei employee works a 12-hour day. New hires sign a "striver agreement" voluntarily surrendering rights to annual leave and overtime pay. Boot-camp style training is standard. The China Labour Bulletin documented that the violent deaths of two employees in close succession brought "wolf culture" under heated public debate, noting one victim was the sixth Huawei employee to die of unnatural causes in recent years, with allegations of 38 such deaths since the company's founding. [Source: analysis]

- Source: [China Labour Bulletin](https://clb.org.hk/en/content/corporate-%E2%80%9Cwolf-culture%E2%80%9D-devouring-china%E2%80%99s-over-worked-employees)
- Source: [Irish Times](https://www.irishtimes.com/business/technology/inside-huawei-s-wolf-culture-of-boot-camp-training-and-high-speed-growth-1.3736700)

### Criticism-and-Self-Criticism Sessions

Former employees describe regular sessions modeled on Maoist "criticism and self-criticism" rituals, which critics call coercive and psychologically damaging. While Ren frames these as tools for organizational learning, external observers note they can suppress dissent and individual agency. [Source: opinion/analysis]

- Source: [China Factor](https://chinafactor.news/2021/01/15/wolf-culture-and-rommel-loom-large-at-huawei/)

### HR Reform Triggered by Internal Revolt

In 2017, an internal HR staff member's post detailing complaints about long working hours, distrust, and dissatisfaction went viral. Ren responded by emailing all employees to apologize, saying "It is the company's mistake, not your problem." This triggered Huawei's "Human Resource Management Outline 2.0" (March 2018), proposing a shift from obedience-based management to trust-based management. Critics note this reform implicitly acknowledged that decades of wolf culture had accumulated serious organizational damage. [Source: analysis]

- Source: [SCMP](https://www.scmp.com/tech/tech-leaders-and-founders/article/3036573/huawei-founder-ren-zhengfei-responds-hr-issues-after)

### Scalability Questions

Some management scholars question whether Ren's highly personal, teacher-mentor leadership style can scale or survive succession. Despite the rotating CEO system, Ren retains veto power, and the question of whether Huawei's culture is transferable without its founder remains open. [Source: analysis]

## 5. How Employees and Former Employees Describe the Experience

**The grind.** One former employee: "I had no time for recreation. Basically, all I did was keep working mindlessly. Often we were still in meetings until 11pm." From the earliest days, managers and employees worked through the night, relying on power naps -- the origin of Huawei's famous "mattress culture." [Source: reported firsthand accounts]

**The rewards.** Employees enrolled in Huawei's virtual restricted stock program receive dividends that can make a longer-tenured, lower-ranking employee earn more than a shorter-tenured, higher-ranking one. About half of Huawei's 190,000+ employees participate. The ESOP requires long-term commitment -- most Huawei employees stay 15-20 years, unprecedented in China tech. Rutgers University's CLEO research unit published a case study calling Huawei a demonstration of "when profit sharing works." [Source: analysis]

**No return policy.** A former HR professional stated: "In general, former Huawei employees are not welcomed back." The culture demands total commitment, and departure is treated as a break in loyalty. [Source: reported firsthand account]

- Source: [Rutgers CLEO](https://cleo.rutgers.edu/articles/huawei-a-case-study-of-when-profit-sharing-works/)
- Source: [NCEO](https://www.nceo.org/employee-ownership-blog/huaweis-phantom-plan-provides-real-ownership-90000-employees)

## 6. International Business Community's Assessment (Management Focus)

### Governance Innovation

The rotating CEO system (launched 2011) -- where three deputy chairmen serve six-month terms as acting CEO -- is widely studied as a governance innovation. Wharton's Knowledge@Wharton analyzed it as a succession planning mechanism, noting Ren's stated motivation: "I don't want Huawei to be clueless about its own business direction if I should pass away." Management Today and the European Business Review both published analyses praising the system's ability to prevent single-point-of-failure leadership while maintaining strategic continuity. However, critics note Ren retains veto power, making the "rotation" more constrained than it appears. [Source: analysis]

- Source: [Wharton Knowledge](https://knowledge.wharton.upenn.edu/article/as-chinese-firms-and-their-ceos-mature-succession-planning-gains-urgency/)
- Source: [European Business Review](https://www.europeanbusinessreview.com/leadership-innovation-huaweis-rotating-ceo-system/)
- Source: [Management Today](https://www.managementtoday.co.uk/why-huawei-swaps-ceo-every-six-months/article/1389976)

### Employee Ownership as Competitive Advantage

Ren's 1.4% personal stake -- with employees holding the rest through virtual restricted shares -- is frequently cited as a radical alternative to founder-dominated tech companies. SSRN academic papers by Toshio Goto analyze the structure's effectiveness, concluding that employee ownership has been "an essential element of Huawei's success." Jeffrey Towson (Peking University) characterizes it as "meritocracy plus partnership at scale." [Source: analysis]

- Source: [SSRN - Who Owns Huawei?](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3856761)
- Source: [Jeffrey Towson Analysis](https://jefftowson.com/2023/10/huaweis-employee-stock-ownership-plan-esop-is-a-great-example-of-meritocracy-plus-partnership-in-china-tech-at-scale-pt-2-of-3/)

### The Paradox Assessment

The international management consensus identifies a central paradox in Ren's approach: he combined genuine employee empowerment (ownership, profit sharing, intellectual engagement) with intense control mechanisms (wolf culture, striver agreements, criticism sessions). The system produced extraordinary results -- Huawei grew from a small reseller to a global technology leader -- but at human costs that Western management norms would consider unacceptable. Whether this paradox is a feature (creative tension) or a flaw (unsustainable contradiction) remains the central debate in management scholarship about Ren Zhengfei. [Source: analysis/synthesis]
